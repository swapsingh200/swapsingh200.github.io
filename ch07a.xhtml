BYGDØY NEIGHBORHOOD

This thought-provoking and exciting cluster of sights, worth ▲▲, is on a parklike peninsula just across the harbor from downtown Oslo. It provides a busy and rewarding half-day (at a minimum) of sightseeing.

Here, within a short walk, are six major sights (listed in order of importance): the Norwegian Folk Museum, an open-air museum with traditional wooden buildings from all corners of the country, a stave church, and a collection of 20th-century urban buildings; the under-construction Museum of the Viking Age, showing off the best-preserved Viking ships in existence (opening in 2027; the Fram Museum, showcasing the modern Viking spirit with the Fram, the ship of Arctic-exploration fame, and the Gjøa, the first ship to sail through the Northwest Passage; the Kon-Tiki Museum, starring the Kon-Tiki and the Ra II, in which Norwegian explorer Thor Heyerdahl proved that early civilizations could have crossed the oceans; the Norwegian Maritime Museum, interesting mostly to old salts; and the Norwegian Holocaust Center, memorializing the Holocaust in Norway.

Getting to Bygdøy: Sailing from downtown to Bygdøy is fun, fast, and gets you in a seafaring mood. Ride the Bygdøy ferry—marked Public Ferry Bygdøy Museums—from pier 3 in front of City Hall (64 NOK one-way, buy ticket online or at the ticket desk, 75 NOK on board, 99 NOK round-trip; covered by Oslo Pass—but not by individual transit tickets; 3/hour, 10-15 minutes; June-Aug daily 9:10-17:50, fewer sailings spring and fall, no boats off-season, https://nyc.no/bygdoyfergen). Boats generally leave from downtown at :10, :30, and :50 past each hour. In summer, avoid the nearby (much more expensive) tour boats.

For a less memorable approach, you can take bus #30 (from the train station or the National Theater, direction: Bygdøy; 20-minute trip).

Returning to Oslo: Note that after 18:00, boat departures back to downtown are sparse, but the #30 bus runs until late. If returning by ferry, get to the dock a little early—otherwise the boat might be full, and you’ll have to wait for the next sailing.

Getting Around Bygdøy: The Norwegian Folk and Viking Age museums are a 10-minute walk from the ferry’s first stop (Dronningen). The other boating museums (Fram, Kon-Tiki, and Maritime) are at the second ferry stop, in an area called Bygdøynes. The Holocaust Center is off Fredriksborgveien, about halfway between these two museum clusters. All Bygdøy sights are within a pleasant (when sunny) 15-minute walk of each other. The walk gives you a picturesque taste of suburban Oslo.

City bus #30 connects the sights four times hourly in this order: Norwegian Folk Museum, Museum of the Viking Age (when open), Kon-Tiki Museum, Norwegian Holocaust Center. (For the Holocaust Center, you’ll use the Bygdøyhus stop a long block away; tell the bus driver you want the stop for the “HL-Senteret.”) The bus turns around at its final stop (Huk), then passes the sights in reverse order on its way back to the city center.

Planning Your Day: If the weather’s good, hit the folk museum first (before it gets too hot). If it’s ugly out, begin with the mostly indoor boat museums at Bygdøynes. The marginally interesting Holocaust Center is a bit of a detour, and skippable if you’re short on time. The Bygdøynes museums offers a discounted combo-ticket if you buy tickets for two or all three at the same time; decide before you buy your first ticket and save a few bucks.

Eating at Bygdøy: Each museum area has $ food options. At the boat museum cluster (Bygdøynes), you’ll find the Framheim Café inside the Fram Museum; a little café window with hot dogs and ice cream outside the Kon-Tiki Museum; and two more-substantial eateries at the Norwegian Maritime Museum: the indoor Kafe Fjord with sandwiches and soup, and the outdoor Fjordterrassen Café, with hot dogs and deep-fried fish and veggie burgers, and tables overlooking the harbor (open May-Aug). The Norwegian Folk Museum houses the decent Kafe Arkadia at its entrance, but picnics are also encouraged. The small Matkroken grocery store close to the Museum of the Viking Age has picnic essentials (bus stop: Fredriksborg). The Holocaust Center has a small café upstairs (closed Mon).

Beach at Bygdøy: A popular beach is located at Huk, on the southwest tip of the peninsula.

Brought from all corners of Norway, 150 buildings have been reassembled here on 35 acres. While Stockholm’s Skansen was the first museum of this kind to open to the public, this collection is a bit older, started in 1881 as the king’s private collection (and the inspiration for Skansen). The folk museum is most lively (and worth ▲▲▲) June through mid-August, when buildings are open and staffed with attendants in period clothing (who’ll happily answer your questions). You’ll also see artisans working at traditional crafts and barnyard animals roaming about. In the off-season, the indoor museum is fine, but the park is just a pleasant place to stroll past lots of locked-up log cabins. Be prepared for an uphill walk to reach the Gol stave church.

Cost and Hours: 180 NOK; daily 10:00-17:00—grounds open until 20:00; Oct-April park open Tue-Sun 11:00-16:00, but most historical buildings closed; Museumsveien 10, bus #30 stops immediately in front, +47 22 12 37 00, www.norskfolkemuseum.no.

Tours and Activities: Upon arrival, pick up the site map and review the list of the day’s events, concerts, and guided tours (on a screen near the ticket desk). In summer, there are usually two guided tours in English per day; the Telemark Farm hosts a small daily fiddle-and-folk-dance show; and a folk music-and-dance show is held each Sunday. If you don’t take a tour, invest in a guidebook and ask questions of the informative attendants stationed in buildings throughout the park.

Visiting the Museum: Think of the visit in three parts: the park sprinkled with old buildings, the re-created old town, and the folk-art museum and other indoor historical exhibits. As you enter, you’ll cut through the courtyard with the museum to reach the open-air sections.

The park is loaded with mostly log-built, sod-roofed cabins from various parts of Norway (arranged roughly geographically). Be sure to go inside the buildings. The evocative Gol stave church, at the top of a hill at the park’s edge, is worth the climb. Built in 1212 in Hallingdal and painstakingly reconstructed here, it has a classic design and, inside, beautiful yet primitive wood paintings on the apse walls (c. 1452). If you won’t make it to a stave church elsewhere on your trip, this is a must. (For more on stave churches, see here.)

Back near the entrance building, the old town has a variety of homes and shops that focus on urban lifestyles. It’s worth exploring the tenement building, where you’ll see intimate, fully furnished apartments from various generations and lifestyles—1905, 1930, 1950, 1979, and even a Norwegian-Pakistani apartment.

In the museum on the courtyard where you entered, the ground floor beautifully presents woody, colorfully painted, and exactingly carved folk art; traditional Norwegian knitting; and weapons. Upstairs are exquisite-in-a-peasant-kind-of-way folk costumes. I’d skip the sleepy collection of Norwegian church art (in an adjoining building). But don’t miss the best Sami culture exhibit I’ve seen in Scandinavia (across the courtyard in the green building, behind the toy exhibit). In the same building find the museum’s newest exhibit, tracing the evolution of Norway’s culture and identity from 1600 to 1914.

▲▲Museum of the Viking Age (Vikingtidsmuseet)

Formerly the Viking Ship Museum, this impressive collection of Viking artifacts (including majestic, well-preserved Viking ships dating from the 9th and 10th centuries) is closed while undergoing a multiyear expansion. It’s scheduled to reopen on the same site in 2027 with triple the space and a new name: the Museum of the Viking Age. In the meantime, a few of the museum’s artifacts are temporarily scattered around Oslo; in particular, the Historical Museum has a good Viking exhibit. For updates on the museum reconstruction, see www.vikingtidsmuseet.no.

▲▲▲Fram Museum (Frammuseet)

GOOGLE MAP

Under its distinctive A-frame roof, this museum holds the 125-foot, steam- and sail-powered ship that took modern-day Vikings Roald Amundsen and Fridtjof Nansen deep into the Arctic and Antarctic, farther north and south than any vessel had gone before. In an adjacent A-frame is Amundsen’s Gjøa, the first ship to sail through the Northwest Passage. Together, the exhibit spins a fascinating tale of adventure, scientific exploration, and human determination...all at subzero temperatures.

Cost and Hours: 140 NOK, combo-ticket possible—see next; daily May-Aug 9:30-18:00, Sept-April 10:00-17:00; Bygdøynesveien 36, +47 23 28 29 50, www.frammuseum.no.

Combo-Tickets: Two combo-ticket options cover the Fram, Kon-Tiki, and Norwegian Maritime museums. One ticket covers two museums of your choice (250 NOK); the other covers all three (380 NOK).

Visiting the Museum: Stepping into the museum, you’re immediately bow-to-bow with the 128-foot Fram. There are two parts to your visit: The Fram, which you’re looking at, and to the left through a tunnel, the smaller Gjøa. I’d see the Gjøa first, because it’s a better lead-up to the main event, and because it features a short film that’s a fine introduction to the entire museum.

Crossing through the tunnel to the Gjøa, you’ll learn about the search for the Northwest Passage (that long-sought-after trade route through the Arctic from the Atlantic to the Pacific). Exhibits tell the story of how Roald Amundsen and a crew of six used this motor- and sail-powered ship to successfully navigate the Northwest Passage (1903-1906). Exhibits describe their ordeal as well as other Arctic adventures, such as Amundsen’s 1925 flight to 88 degrees north (they had to build a runway out of ice to take off and return home); and his 1926 airship (zeppelin) expedition from Oslo over the North Pole to Alaska. This section also features a 100-seat cinema showing an excellent 15-minute film about the exploration of the earth’s polar regions (shows every 15 minutes).

Now return to the Fram and peruse the exhibits—actual artifacts and profiles of the brave explorers and their crew. The upper floor focuses on Nansen’s voyages to the North Pole on the Fram, including an early kayak, a full-size stuffed polar bear, and a model of the ship. Here you can cross a gangway to explore the Fram’s claustrophobic but fascinating interior. A simulated Northern Lights show, best viewed from the Fram’s main deck, is presented every 20 minutes.

The Fram Museum: From Pole to Pole

The Fram Museum tells the tale of two great Norwegian explorers, Fridtjof Nansen and Roald Amundsen.

Early polar explorers focused on the Northwest Passage, believing the Arctic could hold a highly lucrative trade route between the Atlantic and the Pacific oceans. Englishman John Franklin famously led a failed expedition looking for the Northwest Passage in 1845; two ships and 129 men were never seen again. Many others attempted and failed—more than a thousand sailors were lost in the search.

A generation later, with the Northwest Passage conquered by Amundsen, explorers (inspired by tales of Franklin and his ilk) became determined to reach the North Pole.

Fridtjof Nansen (1861-1930) prepared for his North Pole expedition by crossing the inland ice of Greenland in six weeks in 1888—hand-pulling sledges all the way—and then spent the winter learning from Inuit people he met. Nansen also commissioned the Fram, which was purpose-built for Arctic exploration with a rounded hull, designed to withstand being stuck in ice without being crushed. (The downside was that it got tossed around in fierce waves.) It even had a windmill that could power electric lights. In the end, the Fram went on three major expeditions. In each one, the crew set out knowing they’d be living in subzero temperatures for three to five years.

The Fram’s maiden voyage was Nansen’s search for the North Pole (1893-1896). He believed that, due to the natural drifting of the northern ice cap, a ship encased in ice would simply be carried by currents to the North Pole. For three years, the Fram drifted, trapped in the Arctic ice. While he didn’t reach the North Pole, Nansen made better progress than anyone before him.

Then, Otto Sverdrup (1854-1930) took the helm of the Fram and advanced Nansen’s progress (riding icebergs from 1898 until 1902), but he didn’t make it all the way, either.

Finally, Roald Amundsen (1872-1928)—who had already successfully sailed the Northwest Passage—made plans to reach the North Pole in the Fram in 1910. Amundsen’s crew (and financiers) believed he was heading north. But when word came that American explorer Frederick Cook had beaten him to the North Pole, Amundsen sailed the Fram to Antarctica instead and set out to reach the South Pole (1910-1912). Amundsen and his team were in a race to the pole with Englishman Robert Falcon Scott and his men, who had arrived aboard the ship Terra Nova. Amundsen beat Scott to the South Pole by about a month. And, unlike Scott, Amundsen made it home alive.

The Fram expeditions coincided with the nascent Norwegian independence movement and became a proud patriotic symbol of the Norwegian people.

Exhibits on the middle floor focus on Admundsen, who picked up where Nansen left off and explored the South Pole. You’ll see an actual dogsled Admundsen’s team used and a small model of the motorized sled from the rival Scott expedition. Also featured are a tent like the one Amundsen used, reconstructed shelves from his Arctic kitchen, models of the Fram, and a “polar simulator” plunging visitors into a 15° Fahrenheit environment.

▲▲Kon-Tiki Museum (Kon-Tiki Museet)

GOOGLE MAP

Next to the Fram is a museum housing the Kon-Tiki and the Ra II, the ships built by the larger-than-life anthropologist, seafarer, and adventurer Thor Heyerdahl (1914-2002). Heyerdahl and his crew used these ships—constructed entirely without modern technology—to undertake tropical voyages many had thought impossible. Both ships are well displayed and described in English. This museum—more lighthearted than the other boat-focused exhibits—puts you in a castaway mood.

Cost and Hours: 140 NOK, combo-ticket possible—see Fram Museum listing, daily June-Aug 9:30-18:00, Sept-April 10:00-17:00, May 10:00-18:00, Bygdøynesveien 36, +47 23 08 67 67, www.kon-tiki.no.

Background: Thor Heyerdahl believed that early South Americans could have crossed the Pacific to settle Polynesia. To prove his point, in 1947 Heyerdahl and five crewmates constructed the Kon-Tiki raft from balsa wood, using only premodern tools and techniques, and adorned its sail with a giant image of the sun god Kon-Tiki. They set sail from Peru on the tiny craft, surviving for 101 days on fish, coconuts, and sweet potatoes (native to Peru). About 5,000 miles later, they arrived in Polynesia. (While Heyerdahl proved that early South Americans could have made this trip, anthropologists doubt they did.) The Kon-Tiki story became a best-selling book and award-winning documentary (and helped spawn the “Tiki” culture craze in the US). Funded by the Kon-Tiki success, Heyerdahl went on to explore Easter Island (1955) and then turned his attention to another voyage—this time across the Atlantic. In 1970, Heyerdahl’s Ra II made a similar 4,000-mile journey from Morocco to Barbados—on a vessel made of reeds—to prove that Africans could have populated the Americas.

Visiting the Museum: You’ll see both the Kon-Tiki and the Ra II and learn about Heyerdahl’s other adventures (including Easter Island and the Ra I, which sank partway into its journey). Everything is well-described in English and very kid-friendly. In the basement, you’ll see the bottom of the Kon-Tiki vessel with a life-size model of a whale shark (the largest fish on earth) and other marine life that the crew observed at sea. Nearby, a small theater continuously plays a 10-minute documentary about the voyage; every day at 12:00, they show the full-length (67-minute), Oscar-winning 1950 documentary film Kon-Tiki.

▲Norwegian Maritime Museum (Norsk Maritimt Museum)

GOOGLE MAP

This museum provides insight into Norway’s maritime heritage with a special focus on Oslo. Through exhibits covering coastal culture, traditional boats, Viking ship construction, and archaeology, you’ll gain a deeper understanding of this country’s maritime culture and seafaring past.

Cost and Hours: 140 NOK, combo-ticket possible—see Fram Museum listing; daily 10:00-17:00, Oct-March Tue-Sun 11:00-16:00, closed Mon; two cafés—one with city views; Bygdøynesveien 37, +47 22 12 37 00, www.marmuseum.no.

Visiting the Museum: The museum spans two buildings. Starting in the main building, you’ll learn about the Viking age and boat building. The highlight is a small boat from the Gokstad Viking ship discovery. Until the new Museum of the Viking Age opens, this is your best chance in Oslo to see a real Viking boat. Other exhibits include “At Sea,” which takes a personal look at seafarers’ lives from the Viking days to the present. If you appreciate maritime art, the museum’s painting collection will float your boat. But unless you’re a salty sea dog, I’d skip those and head across to the Boat Hall (Båthallen) building. Here you’ll see a collection of traditional boats that capture the challenges and hardships of 19th-century coastal Norway. One floor up are artifacts recently recovered from the many shipwrecks in Oslo’s old harbor illustrating international shipping, trade, and communications from the 15th to the 17th centuries.

Norwegian Holocaust Center (HL-Senteret)

Located in the stately former home of Nazi collaborator Vidkun Quisling—whose name is synonymous with “complicit in atrocities”—this museum and study center offers a high-tech look at the racist ideologies that fueled the Holocaust. It’s designed primarily for Norwegians, but you can borrow a tablet with English translations of the exhibits. The ground floor displays historical documents about the rise of anti-Semitism and personal effects from Holocaust victims. The exhibits continue downstairs, where the names of 760 Norwegian Jews killed by the Nazis are listed in a bright, white room. Out front, the Innocent Questions glass-and-neon sculpture shows an old-fashioned punch card, reminding viewers of how the Norwegian puppet government collected seemingly innocuous information before deporting its Jews.

Cost and Hours: 120 NOK; daily 10:00-18:00, mid-Sept-mid-May until 16:00; Huk Aveny 56—take bus #30 to the Bygdøyhus stop and follow brown HL-Senteret signs, +47 22 84 21 00, www.hlsenteret.no.

GRÜNERLØKKA AND GRØNLAND DISTRICTS

Oslo’s Grünerløkka district, bisected by the Akers River, is lively, and workaday Grønland is emerging as a fun spot. You can connect it all together by following my self-guided Akers River and Grünerløkka Walk (described below).

Akers River

The source of the Akers River (and Oslo’s drinking water) is the pristine Lake Maridal, situated at the edge of the Nordmarka wilderness. The idyllic recreation scenes along Lake Maridal are a favorite for nature-loving Norwegians.

Though only about five miles long, the Akers River powered Oslo’s early industry: flour mills in the 1300s, sawmills in the 1500s, and Norway’s Industrial Revolution in the 1800s. Today the river valley becomes a park as it winds past decent-size waterfalls and red-brick factories that now house cafés and apartments. The bottom of the river—bordered by the high-rise Radisson Blu Plaza Hotel and the “Little Pakistan” neighborhood of Grønland—has its share of rough edges, reflecting a new urban reality in Oslo.

▲Grünerløkka

GOOGLE MAP

The Grünerløkka district, built in the latter half of the 1800s to house the legions of workers employed at factories powered by the Akers River, has reinvented itself as a vibrant neighborhood for shopping, street life, and food. Old-style buildings have found new life as restaurants, bars, and coffeehouses. Vintage shops rub elbows with indie boutiques. Grünerløkka lies just east of the Akers River, offering welcoming green spaces and urban park life.

Getting There: Grünerløkka can be reached from the center of town by a short ride on tram #11, #12, or #18, or by following the walk described next.

▲Akers River and Grünerløkka Walk

This hike up the Akers River, finishing in stylish Grünerløkka, gives a peek at the real (and more diverse) Oslo beyond the touristy central zone. Once housing poor workers, the Grünerløkka neighborhood now attracts Norwegian hipsters and foodie tourists. Allow about 1.5 hours at a brisk pace, including a fair bit of up and down.

In Cold Blood

Norway likes to think of itself as a quiet, peaceful nation on the edge of Europe—after all, its legislators award the Nobel Peace Prize. So, the events of July 22, 2011 had a profound effect on the country’s psyche. On that day, an anti-immigration fanatic named Anders Behring Breivik set off a car bomb in Oslo, killing eight. He then traveled to a summer camp where he hunted down, shot, and killed 69 young people and counselors.

Until Breivik’s attack, Norway had largely escaped 21st-century terrorism. Arrested after the shootings, Breivik was described by police as obsessed with what he saw as the “threat” of multiculturalism and immigration to Norwegian values. More than 15 percent of the population is not ethnic Norwegian, and a quarter of Oslo’s residents are immigrants.

Horrified by Breivik’s actions, after the attack many Oslo natives went out of their way to make their “New Norwegian” neighbors feel welcome.

Norway seems determined not to let the actions of one man poison its peaceful soul. Calls for police to start carrying weapons or to reinstate the death penalty were quickly rejected. “Breivik wanted to change Norway,” an Oslo resident told me. “We’re determined to keep Norway the way it was.”

While there are a few sights along this walk, they’re all skippable—making this a fun experience on a sunny evening after the museums have closed, when Oslo’s young people are out enjoying their riverside park. (Several excellent dining opportunities are along or near this walk.) The walk also works as a bike ride—and there are plenty of Oslo City Bike racks along the way (for details on the city bike program, see here).

Grønland

The T-bane’s Grønland stop deposits you in the center of an old working-class neighborhood of Industrial Age Oslo (if you look down side streets, you’ll see fine 19th-century facades). While a bit shabby, Grønland is emerging as a trendy place for eating out and after-dark fun. Locals know you’ll get double the food and lots more beer for the kroner here. If you’d enjoy a whiff of Istanbul, make a point to wander through the underground commercial zone at the Grønland station (easy to visit even if you’re not riding the T-bane).

SOUTHEAST OF DOWNTOWN

▲Ekeberg Sculpture Park (Ekebergparken)

In 2013, this piece of wilderness—on a forested hill over town, with grand city views—was transformed from a long-neglected public green space into a modern sculpture park. Real estate tycoon and art collector Christian Ringnes (grandson of Norwegian brewery tycoons) paid for the project and wanted his creation to be an homage to women. To that end, the park is filled with modern and contemporary sculptures ranging from the ample nudes of Rodin, Renoir, and Botero to the evocative abstractions of Bourgeois, Turrell, and Holzer.

Getting There: The park, always open and free, is a 10-minute tram ride southeast of the center (at the train station, catch tram #18 from platform E, or #19 from platform C; take either one in the direction of Ljabru). Ride just a few stops to Ekebergparken, right at the park’s entrance. Bus #81 also runs to the park from the city center.

Background: This location (literally “Oak Hill”) is historic: There’s evidence that Stone Age people lived here 7,000 years ago; a spot in this park is said to be where Edvard Munch was inspired to paint The Scream (the viewpoint is marked Utsiktspunkt Skrik on maps), and in World War II, it held a Nazi military cemetery. Today the people of Oslo are embracing this lovely 63-acre mix of forest and contemporary art.

Visiting the Park: A visit here involves lots of climbing on trails through the trees. While some people come for the statues (35 in all, but with plans to expand to 80), others simply enjoy a walk in nature...and most agree that the views of Oslo’s harbor scene (the Opera House, Munch Museum, and Barcode Project) may be the highlight. From the tram stop, hike up to the little cluster of buildings (including the visitors center, where you can pick up a map and join a 1.5-hour guided walk in English—see schedule at www.ekebergparken.com). In this area, you can see the first of the sculptures. For views and more art, keep heading up the hill to the restaurant...and beyond. Maps, suggesting various walking routes, are posted throughout the park.

Nearby: The faint ruins of medieval Oslo (free, always open) are between the harbor and Ekeberg Park. You can get a peek of them as you rumble past on the tram. History buffs can spend a few minutes wandering a park with a few scant foundations of the 11th-century town—back when it was 3,000 people huddled around a big stone cathedral, seat of the Norwegian bishop. The arcade of a 13th-century Dominican monastery still stands (office of today’s Lutheran bishop). To get there, hop off at the Oslo Hospital tram stop; the ruins are across Ekebergveien street from the tram stop.

NORTHWEST OF THE CITY

Oslo is surrounded by a vast forest dotted with idyllic little lakes, huts, joggers, bikers, and sun-worshippers. To sightsee farther afield in this woodsy area, consider these options. For locations, see the “Greater Oslo” map.

▲Holmenkollen Ski Jump and Ski Museum

GOOGLE MAP

The site of one of the world’s oldest ski jumps (from 1892), Holmenkollen has hosted many championships, including the 1952 Winter Olympics. To win the privilege of hosting the 2011 World Ski Jump Championship, Oslo built a bigger jump to match modern ones built elsewhere—futuristic, cantilevered, and Olympic-standard. For skiers and winter-sports fans, a visit here is worth ▲▲.

Cost and Hours: 160-NOK ticket includes museum and viewing platform at top of jump; zip line-690 NOK, ski simulator-110 NOK; daily 9:00-20:00, May and Sept 10:00-17:00, Oct-April until 16:00; +47 22 92 32 64, www.skiforeningen.no/holmenkollen.

Getting There: T-bane line #1 gets you out of the city, through the hills, forests, and mansions that surround Oslo, and to the jump (4/hour, 25 minutes, direction: Frognerseteren). From the Holmenkollen station, you’ll hike steeply up the road 15 minutes to the ski jump. (Getting back down is more like 10 minutes. Note T-bane departure times before you leave.) For more details—including a slightly longer, but more scenic, approach—see “Forest Stroll Near the Ski Jump,” later.

Visiting Holmenkollen: You’ll enter the complex directly under the ramp. First, you’ll tour the ski museum, which traces the evolution of the sport, from 4,000-year-old rock paintings to crude 1,500-year-old wooden sticks to the slick and quickly evolving skis of modern times, including a fun exhibit showing the royal family on skis. You’ll see gear from Roald Amundsen’s famous trek to the South Pole, including the stuffed remains of Obersten (“The Colonel”), one of his sled dogs. Exhibits show off lots and lots of historic skis, gear, and video clips of great moments in skiing. Kids love the downhill ski-breeze simulator...basically a superpowered fan that blasts you in the face with fierce wind.

Near the end of the museum exhibits, look for the sign up to the ski jump. The tilted elevator takes you up to the top (on a sunny day, you may have to wait your turn for the elevator). Stand right at the starting gate, just like an athlete, and get a feel for this daredevil sport. The jump empties into a 30,000-seat amphitheater. Climb the stairs up to the rooftop deck; when it’s clear, you’ll see one of the best possible views of Oslo. While the view is exciting from the top, even more thrilling is the zip line ride that sends daredevils screaming (literally) down over the jump to the landing area at the very bottom in 30 heart-stopping seconds.

As you ponder the jump, consider how modern athletes continually push the boundaries of their sport. The first champion here in 1892 jumped 21 meters (nearly 69 feet). In 1930 it took a 50-meter jump to win. In 1962 it was 80 meters, and in 1980 the champ cracked 100 meters. And, most recently, a jump of 140 meters (459 feet) took first place.

To cap your Holmenkollen experience, you can step into the simulator (or should I say stimulator?) and fly down the ski jump and ski in a virtual downhill race. My legs were exhausted after the five-minute terror. It’s located at the lower level of the complex, near the entry of the ski museum. Outside, have fun watching a candid video of those shrieking inside.

Frognerseteren

Continuing past Holmenkollen on T-bane line #1 to the end of the line at Frognerseteren brings you to the starting point of an easy downhill through the Norwegian forest, with a woodsy coffee or meal break in the middle. At Frognerseteren you can also find mountain biking and (in winter) tobogganing (see “Activities in Oslo,” later).

Forest Stroll Near the Ski Jump: From the Frognerseteren stop, follow signs five minutes downhill on the gravel path to the recommended Frognerseteren Hovedrestaurant, a fine traditional eatery with a sod roof, reindeer meat on the griddle, an affordable cafeteria, and a city view over more sod rooftops (see listing under “Eating in Oslo,” later).

From the restaurant, head down to the little sod-roofed village and look for the Holmenkollen 1.6 sign to the right. Follow the wide, reddish gravel path through the woods (don’t be distracted by the many side-paths, which are for mountain bikers—speaking of which, keep an eye out for bicycles zipping across your path). After about 20 minutes, you’ll come to a paved roller-ski course (use the gravel path alongside), then pass between the wooden Holmenkollen village church and the top of a different ski jump (Midtstubakken). Soon you’ll see the glassy swoop of Holmenkollen on your right; the entrance to the jump is at the base of the ramp.

On your way back to the Holmenkollen T-bane stop, you’ll pass the recommended Holmenkollen Restaurant, with a similar view, but without Frognerseteren’s pewter-and-antlers folk theme—or pretense.

NEAR OSLO

These sights are a bit farther from the city, in opposite directions. Eidsvoll Manor is about 45 minutes north of Oslo, while the fjord town of Drøbak is about an hour south.

▲Eidsvoll Manor

During the Napoleonic period, control of Norway changed from Denmark to Sweden. This ruffled the patriotic feathers of Norway’s Thomas Jeffersons and Ben Franklins, and on May 17, 1814, Norway’s constitution was written and signed in this stately mansion (in the town of Eidsvoll Verk, north of Oslo). While Sweden still ruled, the constitution gave Norway more autonomy. To mark the 2014 bicentennial of Norway’s constitution, the manor building was restored to how it looked in 1814. A visitors center in the nearby Wergeland House uses 21st-century tech to tell the history of Norway’s march to independence (the union with Sweden was dissolved in 1905). Your ticket also includes an English tour, offered daily at 12:30.

Cost and Hours: 140 NOK; daily 10:00-16:00; Sept-April Tue-Fri 11:00-15:00, Sat-Sun until 16:00; +47 63 92 22 10, www.eidsvoll1814.no.

Getting There: By public transportation, take the train to the Eidsvoll Verk station (2/hour, 30 minutes). From there, ride bus #430 three stops (direction: Eidsvoll Station, 5 minutes, included with train ticket) or plan on a 20-minute walk. If you’re driving from Oslo to Lillehammer and the Gudbrandsdal Valley, the manor is right on the way and worth a stop. It’s a 45-minute drive from Oslo: Take road E-6 toward Trondheim, then turn right at the Eidsvolls Bygningen sign. Parking is free.

Drøbak

This delightful fjord town is just an hour from Oslo by bus (61 NOK, ticket covers two zones, 2/hour, bus #500 from behind the train station) or ferry from Aker Brygge (118 NOK one-way, ticket covers four zones, 2/day weekends only spring and fall, also 1/day weekdays mid-June-mid-Aug, take ferry #B21, 1.25 hours, no ferry off-season, check at pier 1 or ask at Oslo TI). Consider taking the boat trip down, exploring the town, having dinner, and taking the bus back. Drøbak TI: +47 48 10 89 92, www.visitdrobak.no.

For holiday cheer year-round, stop into Tregaarden’s Julehus Christmas shop, right off Drøbak’s main square (closed Christmas-Feb, +47 64 93 41 78, www.julehus.no). Then wander out past the church and cemetery on the north side of town to a pleasant park. Looking out into the fjord, you can see the old Oscarsborg Fortress, where Norwegian troops fired cannons and torpedoes to sink Hitler’s warship, Blücher. The attack bought enough time for Norway’s king and parliament to escape capture and eventually set up a government-in-exile in London during the Nazi occupation of Norway (1940-1945). Nearby, a monument is dedicated to the commander of the fortress, and one of Blücher’s anchors rests aground (the other is at Aker Brygge in Oslo). A summer ferry shuttles visitors from the town harbor.

Eating in Drøbak: With outdoor seating, $$$$ Restaurant Skipperstuen is a pricey but good option for dinner overlooking the fjord and all the Oslo-bound boat traffic (Mon-Sat 12:00-22:00, Sun until 20:00; off-season closed Mon-Tue; Havnebakken 11, +47 64 93 07 03, www.skipperstuen.no).

Activities in Oslo

Oslofjord Island Beaches

On a hot day, the busy ferry scene at Oslo’s harborfront lures locals out to the cool, green islands across the harbor where they can take a dip in the fjord, enjoy a little beach time, or simply stroll and enjoy views of the city. (You can reach several of Oslofjord’s islands on my self-guided ferry tour, earlier in this chapter.) Hovedøya island (five minutes from town by ferry) has a nice beach and interesting abbey ruins. Little Gressholmen has good swimming, a rustic café, and easy wandering to a pair of connected islands. Farther out is Langøyene (“the Long Islands”), once two islands that are now connected. Out here is another popular beach, and a big space for ballgames.

Getting to the Islands: From Aker Brygge, catch ferry #B1 or #B2 to Hovedøya or Gressholmen. Langøyene is only served by #B2 (both routes are covered by public transit tickets and Oslo Pass). For schedules and fare info, check Ruter.no.

Huk Beach

At the tip of the Bygdøy peninsula, Huk beach is a popular spot for sunbathing. Take bus #30 (from near the National Theater or from other Bygdøy sights) to the Huk stop. Once there, to your left is a clothed beach (and to the right is a nude beach).

Swimming

Oslo is dotted with swimming spots along its downtown harbor. On the east side of the harbor, there’s a small beach and dock at the end of the Sørenga neighborhood (past the Munch Museum, near the ruins of medieval Oslo). For families with small kids, head to the beach just next to the Opera House. On the other (west) side of the city, at the tip of Tjuvholmen is a small floating dock with a diving board, packed with locals on warm days.

Tøyenbadet, a modern indoor/outdoor pool complex with a 330-foot-long waterslide, also has a gym and sauna (northeast of downtown at Helgengate 90, +47 23 30 44 70). Oslo’s free botanical gardens are nearby.

One of the easiest escapes beyond the city center is simply to grab a beach towel and ride T-bane line #5 to its last stop, Sognsvann, and join the lakeside scene of Norwegians at play. A pleasant trail leads around the lake. This is also a great area for mountain biking (see “Mountain Biking,” later).

Floating Saunas

The latest rage in Oslo is floating saunas. Locals relax in the hot and steamy boxes before taking a plunge in the fjord, followed by a little sunbathing. You’ll see these mini spas—open year-round—moored like boats all along the harborfront. To enjoy the experience, book a 90-minute timeslot online in advance. While some spas are small and simple, others are a woody wonderland of saunas, sunbathing perches, and ladders into the water. This is a great way to interact with locals in an informal setting (generally 200 NOK, drop-ins welcome if there’s space, bring your own swimsuit and two towels—one to sit on and one to dry yourself off, cold showers on-site, outside drinks allowed, see websites below for details).

My favorite floating sauna is at Sukkerbiten (the Sugar Cube), with nine different saunas, the original floating sauna, and the flagship spa of the Oslo Sauna Association (just beyond the Munch museum at Nylandsveien 28, more across from the Opera House along Langkaia, www.oslobadstuforening.no). Another option is KOK, which offers motorized sauna-boats, allowing you to soak and cruise the Oslofjord simultaneously (at Langkaia and Aker Brygge, www.koknorge.no). Farther along Langkaia is SALT, with large public saunas and smaller private saunas for rent. Note that at SALT you cannot jump into the fjord, but they have large cold-plunge tubs and access to a bar (Langkaia 1, www.salted.no).

Kayaking on the Akers River

Several outfits run kayak tours on the Akers River. For details, see “Tours in Oslo,” earlier in this chapter.

Toboggan Run

In wintertime, hop on T-bane line #1 and ride to the end of the line to Frognerseteren. From here, a short, snowy downhill walk takes you to the Oslo Sledding Association’s clubhouse, which rents sleds and equipment. You’ll gear up and join locals young and old as they sled down the 1.2-mile-long Korketrekkeren (corkscrew) slope. The ride takes about 8-10 minutes and ends at the Midtstuen T-bane stop, from where you can ride back to Frognerseteren and repeat (130 NOK/day, in winter Mon-Fri 10:00-21:00, Sat from 9:00, Sun until 18:00, check online in advance for snow conditions—usually best in the morning, akeforeningenoslo@gmail.com, www.akeforeningen.no).

Mountain Biking

Mountain biking possibilities are endless (as you’ll discover if you go exploring without a good map). Consider taking your bike on the T-bane (free outside of rush hour, 16 NOK at other times) to the end of line #1 to Frognerseteren to gain the most altitude. Then follow the gravel roads (mostly downhill but with some climbs) past several dreamy lakes to Sognsvann, at the end of T-bane line #5. Farther east, from Maridalsvannet, a bike path follows the Akers River all the way back into town. Get biking details at the TI.

Amusement Park

The giant Tusenfryd amusement complex, just outside of town, offers a world of family fun. It’s like a Norwegian Disneyland and Viking Knott’s Berry Farm, with more than 50 rides, plenty of entertainment, and restaurants (closed off-season, take regional bus #500 from behind Oslo’s train station, +47 64 97 66 99, www.tusenfryd.no).

Shopping in Oslo

Shops in Oslo are generally open 10:00-18:00 or 19:00. Many close early on Saturday and all day Sunday. Shopping centers have similar hours without the Saturday early closure. Here are a few favorite shopping suggestions (note all of these are closed on Sunday). For locations, see the “Oslo Center Hotels” map, later.

Norway Designs, just off Karl Johans Gate, shows off the country’s sleek, contemporary designs in clothing, kitchenware, glass, textiles, jewelry—and high prices (Lille Grensen 7, T-bane: Stortinget, +47 23 11 45 10).

Paleet is a mall in the heart of Oslo, with 30 shops on three levels and a food court in the basement (Karl Johans Gate 37, +47 23 08 08 11).

Dale of Norway, considered Norway’s biggest and best maker of traditional and contemporary sweaters, offers its complete collection at their concept store in downtown Oslo (Karl Johans Gate 45, +47 97 48 12 07).

Heimen Husfliden has a superb selection of authentic Norwegian-made sweaters, bunads (national costumes), yarn, traditional jewelry, and colorful Norwegian folk crafts (top quality at high prices, within Hotell Bondeheimen at Arbeidergata 1, +47 23 21 42 00).

GlasMagasinet is one of Oslo’s oldest and fanciest department stores (top-end, good souvenir shop, near the cathedral at Stortorvet 9, +47 22 82 23 00). In the basement of the GlasMagasinet is another branch of Heimen Husfliden (listed earlier, +47 22 42 10 75).

Byporten, the big, splashy mall adjoining the central train station, is filled with youthful and hip shops, specialty stores, and eateries (Jernbanetorget 6, +47 23 36 21 60).

Bogstadveien is a street that’s considered to have the city’s trendiest boutiques and chic, high-quality shops. (It stretches from behind the Royal Palace to Majorstuen near Vigeland Sculpture Park.)

Oslo’s Flea Market makes Saturday morning a happy day for those who brake for garage sales (at Vestkanttorvet, March-Nov only, two blocks east of Frogner Park at the corner of Professor Dahl’s Gate and Neubergsgate).

Oslo Flaggfabrikk sells quality flags of all shapes and sizes, including the long, pennant-shaped vimpel, seen fluttering from flagpoles all over Norway (Stortingsgata 30, +47 22 40 50 60).

Vinmonopolet stores are the only places where you can buy wine and spirits in Norway. (Beer with alcohol content up to 4.7 percent is sold in grocery stores, but stronger beer is limited to Vinmonopolet shops.) In Oslo the most convenient location is at the central train station. Other locations are at Rosenkrantz Gate 11 and inside the Paleet mall (Karl Johans Gate 37).

Entertainment in Oslo

Opera and Ballet Performances

The Norwegian National Opera and Ballet presents a top-notch program of classical and contemporary performances Aug-June at the gleaming-white Opera House on the harborfront at Bjørvika. For tickets, visit the box office Mon-Sat 11:00-16:00, Sun 12:00-16:00, call + 47 21 42 21 21, or go to Operaen.no/en. Guided tours in English are offered daily in summer (see here).

Classical Music

Founded in 1919, the renowned Oslo Philharmonic Orchestra offers a rich program of classical music (including guest performers) at the Oslo Concert Hall (Aug-May, Munkedamsveien 14). For ticket information call +47 23 11 60 60 or visit Ofo.no/en.

Live Theater

The National Theater, Norway’s largest theater company, stages an eclectic program Aug-mid-June at the stately National Theater (along Karl Johans Gate near the Royal Palace) and at other venues around town. Guided tours of the main theater are offered in English several days a week during summer (for info, tickets, and tours, visit http://www.nationaltheatret.no).

Movies

The domed Colosseum Kino in Majorstuen, with 1,500 seats, is a fun place to catch a big-time spectacle. Originally built in 1928, this now high-tech, four-screen theater shows first-run films in their original language (Fridtjof Nansens Vei 6, a short walk west from Majorstuen T-bane station, www.oslokino.no).

Live Music

Oslo has a handful of good places with live music (for locations, see the “Oslo Center Restaurants” map, later). The Dubliner is an Irish pub that hosts live traditional sessions every Tuesday and Saturday (open daily until late, Rådhusgata 28, www.dubliner.no). Herr Nilsen Jazz Club, two blocks off Karl Johans Gate, is a local favorite for lively performances and an enthusiastic crowd. Arrive an hour early to get a seat (daily until late, C.J. Hambros Plass 5, www.herrnilsen.no). Queens Pub is a casual sports bar with free live music (of varying quality) nightly starting at 22:30 (daily until late, close to the train station at Brugata 14, www.queenspuboslo.no). John Dee and Rockefeller Music Hall are larger venues right next to one another. Both host regular concerts by Norwegian and international artists (book tickets online in advance, Mariboes Gate 1, www.rockefeller.no).

Festivals

Oslo hosts several annual music festivals, many of them in late summer. The largest is Øyafestivalen, with a wide range of artists (early Aug, www.oyafestivalen.no). By:Larm is one of Oslo’s most important music festivals, featuring up-and-coming artists (mid-Sept, www.bylarm.no). Kirkemusikkfestivalen, Oslo’s international Church Music Festival, happens twice a year and offers a lineup including Gregorian performances, Baroque pieces, and jazz and contemporary folk music (mid-March and early-Sept, www.kirkemusikkfestivalen.no). Melafestivalen is a multicultural festival and one of the largest of its kind in Europe. This celebration of music, food, dance, and crafts takes place on Rådhusplassen (late Aug, www.mela.no). The Oslo Jazz Festival spans several venues (late Aug, www.oslojazz.no). The OverOslo music festival takes place northeast of the city in a scenic outdoor space overlooking the city (mid-June, www.overoslo.no).

Sleeping in Oslo

Oslo’s hotel rates are particularly susceptible to supply-and-demand fluctuation. You may find deals on weekends and during some parts of the summer, but even then, conventions or special meetings can cause hotel prices to double overnight.

Beyond standard hotels, Oslo is a good city for rooms in private homes and apartments offered through services such as Airbnb or www.bbnorway.com. When selecting a room, consider that sleeping downtown puts you close to the sights, but may come with noise (many loud nightclubs are in the center). The upscale area behind the Royal Palace (Frogner and Majorstuen neighborhoods) gives you a posh address. And for proximity to a trendy dining scene, look in the part of Grünerløkka near the river.

Eating in Oslo

Dining out in Oslo is not cheap, but there are ways to eat well for a reasonable price. I find that a mediocre meal can be overpriced, but a fine meal can be surprisingly affordable. In other words, if you’re eating in a restaurant at all, you may as well splurge for higher quality. I’d picnic or eat at a café or bakery for lunch and spring for a memorable dinner in a serious restaurant. (Remember that tax and tip are included in menu prices, you can share dishes, and water is free with a smile.)

A few tips: Middag refers to dinner, and reservations are smart for any upscale restaurant (seats at the bar and outside may be available for drop-ins). Waiters are well paid, and a 5-10 percent tip at a restaurant (if you enjoyed the food and service) goes a long way.

Groceries are inexpensive in supermarkets (often filling the basement level of departments stores). Smaller grocery stores are commonplace and typically open long hours daily. You’ll find picnic-ready salads, sandwiches, and yogurt/granola tubs with collapsible spoons. Or grab some Wasa crackers and meat, shrimp, or cheese spread in a tube; these are cheap and pack well.

Norwegian Cuisine

Traditional Norwegian cuisine rarely inspires enthusiasm among foodies. But the typical diet of meat, fish, and potatoes is evolving to incorporate more diverse products, and the food here is steadily improving. Fresh produce, colorful markets, and efficient supermarkets abound in Europe’s most expensive corner.

In this land of farmers and fishermen, traditional recipes are built on ingredients like potatoes, salmon, or beef. A favorite tart-and-sweet condiment is lingonberry jam, which cuts through a heavy, salty meat dish. Some traditional places also serve lefse—a soft flatbread made from potatoes, milk, and flour.

Norway’s national dish is Fårikål, a lamb or mutton stew with cabbage, peppercorns, and potatoes. This dish is so popular that the last Thursday in September is Fårikål day in Norway. There’s really no need for a recipe, so every stew turns out differently—and every grandma claims hers is the best.

Because of its long, cold winters, Norway relies heavily on the harvesting and preservation of fish. Smoked salmon, called røkt laks, is prepared by salt-curing the fish and cold-smoking it, ensuring the temperature never rises above 85°F. This makes the texture smooth and almost raw. Bacalao is another favorite: salted and dried cod that is rehydrated and rinsed in water before cooking. You’ll often find bacalao served with tomatoes and olives. Stockfish (tørrfisk), usually cod, is hung on wooden racks to air-dry (rather than salt-cure), creating a kind of “fish jerky” that goes well with beer. Klippfisk is yet another variation, which is partly air-dried on rocks along the shoreline, then further cured in salt.

Some Norwegians serve lutefisk around Christmas time, but you won’t find this salty, pungent dish on the menu at other times of year. Instead, try the more pleasant fiskekake, a small white fish cake made with cream, eggs, milk, and flour, on offer year-round.

For a break from seafood, try local specialties such as reindeer meatballs, or pork-and-ground beef meat cakes called kjøttkaker. True to Scandinavian cuisine, kjøttkaker are usually slathered in a heavy cream sauce served with mushy peas, potatoes, and lingonberry jam.

Dessert and coffee after a meal are essential. Bløtkake, a popular delight on Norway’s Constitution Day (May 17), is a layered cake drizzled with strawberry juice, covered in whipped cream, and decorated with fresh strawberries. The cloudberry (multe), which grows in the Scandinavian tundra, makes a unique jelly that tastes delicious on vanilla ice cream, or even whipped into a rich cream topping for heart-shaped waffles. Norwegians are proud of their breads and pastries, and you’ll never be too far from a bakery that sells an almond-flavored kringle or a cone-shaped krumkake cookie.

You can eat for around 200 NOK at any of these spots, and grab a drink from the container serving a wonderland of local beers and cocktails. BY PLANE

Oslo Airport

Oslo Lufthavn, also called Gardermoen, is about 30 miles north of the city center and has a helpful 24-hour information center (code: OSL, www.osl.no).

The fastest and cheapest way to get into the city center is by train. Exiting baggage claim, follow signs to the train. Here you’ll see two options: local trains (Vy) take about 25 minutes to downtown (118 NOK, 2-3/hour, cheaper with an Oslo Pass, buy ticket at machines or counter, or pay 40 NOK extra on the train); the private, competing Flytoget Airport Express train is a few minutes faster but costs nearly double (230 NOK, 4/hour, runs roughly 5:00-24:00, www.flytoget.no; swipe your credit card at the gate and choose your destination station). Unless you’re in a big hurry or enjoy paying double, I’d stick with the Vy trains. Note that for either option, trains go to Oslo S (the main train station). Some departures continue through the city, stopping at the National Theater station (closer to most recommended hotels). Unfortunately, this isn’t clearly indicated; to spare yourself a transfer (or save some extra walking), ask whether your train continues to the National Theater.

Flybuss airport buses stop directly outside the arrival hall and make several downtown stops, including the central train station (229 NOK one-way, 3/hour, 40 minutes, +47 67 98 04 80)—but since it costs more than the train and takes twice as long, it’s hard to justify (www.flybussen.no).

Taxis take about 45 minutes to downtown and vary in price depending on the company; figure 800-1100 NOK on weekdays, more on weekends or after hours. A taxi-booking machine at the information center in the arrivals hall lets you comparison-shop prices and order a cab. Tipping is not necessary.

Sandefjord Airport Torp

Ryanair, WizzAir, and other discount airlines use this airport 70 miles south of Oslo (code: TRF, www.torp.no). You can take a bus into downtown Oslo (340 NOK, www.torpekspressen.no). Buses to Sandefjord depart Oslo’s central bus terminal (next to the train station) about three hours before all flight departures.

Consider connecting Oslo and Copenhagen by DFDS cruise ship. The boat leaves daily from Oslo around 15:00 and arrives in Copenhagen about 10:00 the following morning (return sailing from Copenhagen follows the same schedule; about 19 hours each way). The boat leaves Oslo from the tip of the Akershus Fortress peninsula (take bus #60 from the train station to the Vippetangen stop and follow signs to DFDS ticket office at Akershusstranda 31). For more specifics and sample prices, see “By Overnight Boat to Oslo” on here.

You can also take the same DFDS cruise ship overnight from Oslo to Frederikshavn, on Denmark’s Jutland peninsula—a great option for drivers. For the best prices, book in advance online or by email (www.dfds.com, dkinfo@dfds.com).
